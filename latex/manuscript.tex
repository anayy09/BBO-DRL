\begin{abstract}
Task offloading in IoMT networks creates a three-way conflict between
end-to-end latency, wearable battery life, and the routing information
that leaks to a passive observer. We present BBO-DRL, a hybrid
scheduler that pairs a Deep Q-Network with a Bombardier Beetle
Optimizer to route tasks across a four-tier hierarchy: wearable
devices, an edge gateway, fog nodes, and a cloud server. The DQN
encodes a six-dimensional state and pre-filters the action space to its
top three candidate nodes; the BBO then searches within that subspace
using chemical-spray exploration and predator-avoidance exploitation.
Three CI-adaptive weight functions shift the energy--latency--privacy
trade-off as patient acuity changes, and the privacy cost term is a
Shannon-entropy penalty on the empirical routing distribution. We
evaluated the system on four open datasets---Mendeley IoMT (50{,}000
records), MIT-BIH Arrhythmia (8{,}640 windows), CICIoMT2024, and
MedSec-25 (554{,}534 flows)---using 30 Monte Carlo replicates per cell
across task scales of $N \in \{100, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000\}$. At
$N = 1000$, BBO-DRL produced the lowest privacy risk among adaptive
methods ($0.322 \pm 0.015$, versus $0.398$ for PSO and HS-HHO and
$0.681$ for ACO; all $p < 0.001$ after Bonferroni correction). It did
not lead on latency or energy: PSO and HS-HHO reached 65.5--65.8~ms
at 5.60--5.69~mJ per task, against BBO-DRL's 74.9~ms and 7.36~mJ.
The gap narrowed to 3.8~ms at $N = 5000$ as the DQN policy converged,
but PSO retained a modest advantage at every scale. Two ablation
baselines locate where the benefit comes from: BBO-only matched PSO on
every metric, and DQN-only never recovered from cold-start exploration
($231.9$~ms, $6.25\%$ SLA violations at $N = 1000$). On the MIT-BIH
real-trace evaluation, BBO-DRL was the only adaptive method to
diversify routing (privacy risk $0.451$ versus $0.81$--$0.90$ for
stateless baselines that collapsed entirely onto the cloud server).
The MedSec-25 traffic-analysis guard achieved precision $0.999$,
recall $0.967$, and AUC $0.995$ at the chosen entropy threshold.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% =======================================================================

A wearable ECG patch generating 360 samples per second creates roughly
5~KB of data per ten-second analysis window. Running the arrhythmia
detection model locally on an ESP32-S3 drains the 300~mAh coin cell in
under six hours. Sending every window to a cloud server adds 50--80~ms
of round-trip latency, which is tolerable for billing pipelines but not
for a patient who just entered ventricular fibrillation. This is the
core tension in IoMT task scheduling, and it does not yield to simple
rules.

Clinical wearables today span ECG patches, pulse oximeters, continuous
blood pressure cuffs, and accelerometer-based fall detectors. Each
produces data at different rates, with different computational demands,
and with different tolerance for delay~\cite{moody2001mitbih}. The
standard clinical SLA for ECG anomaly detection sits around 150~ms;
activity-recognition tasks can wait 500~ms without consequence. Most
deployed systems use one of two strategies: run everything locally
(fast but battery-limited), or forward everything to the cloud
(flexible but slow). Both ignore the middle tier entirely.

Edge gateways such as the Raspberry Pi 4 sustain 1.5~GHz computation
at 3.4~W and sit physically close to the patient~\cite{rpi4_specs}.
Fog nodes extend this capacity further with dedicated NICs and more
RAM. The architectural opportunity is real. The problem is deciding,
for each incoming task, which tier to use. That decision must happen in
milliseconds and must account for current channel conditions, battery
state, queue depth at each node, and the patient's clinical severity
right now. That last factor is the hard part. When a patient
transitions from stable to critical, the priorities invert: energy
conservation becomes irrelevant, latency becomes paramount, and the
system must route aggressively even at the cost of some privacy
exposure.

Classical swarm algorithms---PSO, ACO, WOA variants---solve the
routing problem at a snapshot in time, then restart from scratch on
the next task. They carry no memory of what worked before. When
the network congests or a patient deteriorates rapidly, they converge
to suboptimal solutions because their fitness landscape changes faster
than they can track. Pure deep reinforcement learning (DRL) approaches
learn temporal patterns but face a dimensionality problem: every fog
node, bandwidth allocation level, and encryption option expands the
action space and slows convergence. Neither family alone fits the
clinical deployment scenario~\cite{mnih2015}.

BBO-DRL addresses these shortcomings directly. A Deep Q-Network
provides environment-aware state embedding and pre-filters the action
space to $K$ candidate nodes, reducing the search to a manageable
subspace. The Bombardier Beetle Optimizer~\cite{bbo2025} then searches
within that subspace using chemical-spray exploration and
predator-avoidance exploitation. The BBO was introduced in 2025 and
outperforms CDO, BTO, PSO, and GSA on the CEC~2017 benchmark suite;
this paper is the first application of BBO to healthcare task
offloading. Scheduling decisions are continuously reweighted by a
Patient Criticality Index (CI), which maps raw physiological features
to a scalar $\Phi_i \in [0,1]$ using a model trained on 50{,}000
Mendeley IoMT records~\cite{mendeley_iomt} and explained via
SHAP~\cite{shap2017}. The architecture was originally disclosed as a
patent application~\cite{patent2024}; this paper formalizes that
architecture with mathematical modeling and full empirical validation
on four open datasets, replacing the patent's ACO/PSO claims with
the hybrid BBO-DRL formulation.

The contributions of this work are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A hybrid BBO-DRL scheduler in which a CI-modulated DQN reward
        function shapes exploration and the BBO performs local search
        within the DQN's top-$K$ candidate subspace. Two ablation
        baselines---BBO-only and DQN-only---isolate the contribution
        of each component and quantify the cost of removing it.
  \item An entropy-based privacy cost term integrated directly into the
        scheduling objective, producing a continuous signal that pushes
        the scheduler to diversify routing under high $\rho_i$. An
        explicit traffic-analysis detection rule built on the same
        routing entropy is evaluated on MedSec-25 with full TP/FP
        characterisation.
  \item Three CI-to-weight mappings (exponential energy decay,
        normalised-exponential latency growth, polynomial privacy
        relaxation) with an ablation against flat, step, and linear
        schemes on the same scheduler. The ablation result is negative
        on a mixed-CI workload and is reported as such.
  \item A SHAP-explained CI module trained on the 50{,}000-record
        Mendeley IoMT dataset, with a feature importance ordering that
        matches sepsis-screening clinical intuition.
  \item A full empirical evaluation: 30-replicate Monte Carlo runs at
        five task scales ($N \in \{100, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000\}$) on
        synthetic mixed-CI workloads, plus an 8{,}640-task real-trace
        evaluation on the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database. The comparison
        includes a PSO+DQN hybrid that uses the same DQN architecture
        as BBO-DRL but substitutes PSO for the BBO inner search,
        directly testing whether BBO specifically or the DRL+bio-inspired
        coupling structure drives the privacy advantage. All pairwise
        comparisons against BBO-DRL are tested with Wilcoxon rank-sum
        and Bonferroni correction.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Bio-Inspired Scheduling for Edge Offloading}

PSO and ACO have been the workhorses of MEC task scheduling for nearly
a decade. They converge reliably under static conditions---fixed
topology, predictable workloads, slowly changing channel gains. The
WOA-based scheduler of Chen~et~al.~\cite{chen2022woa} achieved roughly
15\% better energy efficiency than standard PSO on edge scheduling
tasks, a genuine improvement, though the evaluation used synthetic
Gaussian workloads and the advantage narrowed once the authors
introduced variable-rate ECG payloads in a sensitivity test.

More recent hybrids pair two bio-inspired algorithms to compensate for
individual weaknesses. The HS-HHO framework of Liu~et~al.~\cite{liu2023hshho}
merges Slime Mould Algorithm with Harris Hawks Optimization; LITO~\cite{kaveh2024lito}
takes inspiration from lemur huddling. Both achieve competitive results
at 10--20 edge nodes. Their shared limitation is that they are purely
reactive: the fitness function evaluates from scratch each call, and no
information about past routing decisions carries forward. In a ten-minute
patient monitoring session with thousands of micro-tasks, this means the
optimizer re-discovers the same solution structure repeatedly, paying
convergence cost each time.

The BBO algorithm~\cite{bbo2025}, introduced in 2025, handles the
exploration-exploitation balance differently from earlier insect- and
bird-inspired methods. Its chemical-spray phase uses a radius that
decays quadratically over iterations, enabling aggressive early
exploration and precise convergence near the optimum. Published
CEC~2017 results show it outperforming PSO, GSA, BTO, and CDO across
30 benchmark functions. Its application to healthcare task offloading
is new.

\subsection{DRL-Based Task Offloading}

DQN and PPO have both been applied to MEC scheduling with measurable
gains over heuristic approaches~\cite{he2021drl}. The core advantage
is temporal coherence: the agent learns that an edge node with
consistently low queue depth is worth routing to even when its
instantaneous compute speed looks marginal. Stateless optimizers cannot
see that.

Wang~et~al.~\cite{wang2023attn} showed that a DRL agent with an
attention mechanism reduces SLA violations by 22\% over rule-based
heuristics in a 15-node deployment. The cost is pre-training: their
model required roughly 50{,}000 task events before the policy
stabilized. In IoMT environments, where the task distribution from
cardiac patients differs substantially from post-surgical recovery
patients, transferring a pre-trained policy to a new ward without
retraining remains an open problem.

Pure DRL also does not scale gracefully when the action space grows.
Adding three fog nodes to a five-node deployment does not triple the
state space, but it substantially enlarges the Q-value table and
slows convergence. We address this directly: the BBO pre-filters the
action space to $K = 3$ candidates before the DQN policy update,
so the DQN handles temporal learning while the BBO handles
combinatorial search within a fixed-size subspace.

\subsection{Privacy-Aware Offloading in Healthcare IoT}

Differential privacy and federated learning protect health data at the
model level~\cite{abadi2016dp}, but they address a different threat
than the one we target. Federated learning protects model weights; it
says nothing about traffic-analysis attacks. A device that consistently
offloads to the same edge node leaks information about the patient's
acuity state through flow metadata alone---inter-arrival time and
payload size, without decrypting a single byte~\cite{goldreich2001comp}.

Shannon entropy over the historical offloading distribution is a direct
measure of this leakage: low entropy means the routing pattern is
predictable, and predictability translates to information available to
a passive observer. Niu~et~al.~\cite{niu2023entropy} used
entropy-based metrics for privacy evaluation in MEC, but as a post-hoc
analysis rather than as part of the scheduling objective itself. We
incorporate $R_P$ directly into the cost function, so the scheduler
actively learns to diversify routing under high-privacy-sensitivity
conditions.

\section{System Model}
\label{sec:system_model}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Network and Task Definitions}
\label{subsec:network}

We consider a four-tier heterogeneous healthcare network. Let
\begin{align}
  \set{U} &= \{u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_N\}, \label{eq:set_U}\\
  \set{E} &= \{e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_M\}, \label{eq:set_E}\\
  \set{C} &= \{c\},                      \label{eq:set_C}
\end{align}
denote the set of $N$ IoMT wearable devices, $M$ edge/fog compute nodes
(one RPi4 edge gateway plus $M-1$ fog servers), and one cloud server,
respectively. Wearables communicate over 20~MHz Wi-Fi; edge-to-fog
links use 5G backhaul at 100~MHz.

A healthcare task generated by $u_i \in \set{U}$ at time $t_i$ is
described by the 4-tuple
\begin{equation}
  \tau_i = \left(D_i,\; C_i,\; T^{\max}_i,\; \rho_i\right),
  \label{eq:task_tuple}
\end{equation}
where $D_i$~(bits) is the data payload, $C_i$~(CPU cycles) is the
required compute work, $T^{\max}_i$~(s) is the clinical SLA deadline
(e.g., 150~ms for ECG anomaly detection, 500~ms for activity
recognition), and $\rho_i \in [0,1]$ is the privacy sensitivity level.

For each task $\tau_i$, the scheduler selects an execution node via
\begin{equation}
  x_i \;\in\; \{0,\, 1,\, 2,\, \ldots,\, M+2\},
  \label{eq:decision_var}
\end{equation}
where $x_i=0$ is local execution, $x_i=1$ is the edge gateway,
$x_i \in \{2,\ldots,M+1\}$ are fog nodes, and $x_i=M+2$ is cloud.

\subsection{Latency Model}
\label{subsec:latency}

\subsubsection{Local execution}
\begin{equation}
  T^{\mathrm{local}}_i = \frac{C_i}{f_w},
  \label{eq:latency_local}
\end{equation}
where $f_w$~(cycles/s) is the wearable CPU frequency (240~MHz for the
ESP32-S3).

\subsubsection{Wireless uplink rate}
Applying Shannon's capacity theorem to the uplink channel between $u_i$
and compute node $j$:
\begin{equation}
  R_{i,j} = B \log_2\!\left(1 + \frac{P^t_i \, h_{i,j}}{\sigma^2 + I_j}\right),
  \label{eq:shannon_rate}
\end{equation}
where $B$~(Hz) is the channel bandwidth, $P^t_i$~(W) is the
transmission power, $\sigma^2$~(W) is the AWGN noise power, and $I_j$
is co-channel interference at node $j$~\cite{rappaport2002}.

\subsubsection{Path-loss channel gain}
\begin{equation}
  h_{i,j} = h_0 \left(\frac{d_0}{d_{i,j}}\right)^{\!\alpha},
  \label{eq:channel_gain}
\end{equation}
with reference distance $d_0$, reference gain $h_0$, distance $d_{i,j}$
(m), and path-loss exponent $\alpha \in [2,4]$ ($\alpha=3$ for indoor
clinical environments).

\subsubsection{Queueing delay}
Task arrivals at each compute node follow a Poisson process with rate
$\lambda_j$; service times are exponentially distributed with rate
$\mu_j$, forming an M/M/1 queue~\cite{kleinrock1975}. Expected waiting
time is
\begin{equation}
  T^{\mathrm{queue}}_{i,j} = \frac{\lambda_j}{\mu_j(\mu_j - \lambda_j)},
  \quad \lambda_j < \mu_j.
  \label{eq:queue_delay}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Total offloading latency}
\begin{equation}
  T^{\mathrm{offload}}_{i,j} =
    \frac{D_i}{R_{i,j}}
    + \frac{d_{i,j}}{v_s}
    + T^{\mathrm{queue}}_{i,j}
    + \frac{C_i}{f_j},
  \label{eq:latency_offload}
\end{equation}
where $v_s \approx 3\times10^8$~m/s and $f_j$ is the CPU frequency of
node $j$. The end-to-end task latency under decision $x_i$ is then
\begin{equation}
  T_i(x_i) =
  \begin{cases}
    T^{\mathrm{local}}_i            & x_i = 0, \\[3pt]
    T^{\mathrm{offload}}_{i,\,x_i} & x_i \geq 1.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:latency_total}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Energy Model}
\label{subsec:energy}

Energy is modeled from the wearable's perspective, as battery life is
the binding hardware constraint in continuous patient monitoring.

\subsubsection{Local computation (CMOS)}
\begin{equation}
  E^{\mathrm{local}}_i = \kappa \, C_i \, f_w^2,
  \label{eq:energy_local}
\end{equation}
where $\kappa$~(J\,s$^2$/cycle$^3$) is the effective switched
capacitance~\cite{burd1995processor}. For the ESP32-S3,
$\kappa = 10^{-27}$~F/cycle$^2$.

\subsubsection{Offloading energy}
During offloading the wearable is active for $T^{\mathrm{tx}}_{i,j} = D_i/R_{i,j}$
and idle for the remainder:
\begin{equation}
  E^{\mathrm{offload}}_i =
    P^t_i \cdot T^{\mathrm{tx}}_{i,j}
    + P_{\mathrm{idle}} \cdot \!\left(T^{\mathrm{offload}}_{i,j} - T^{\mathrm{tx}}_{i,j}\right),
  \label{eq:energy_offload}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\mathrm{idle}} = 33$~mW is the ESP32-S3 quiescent power draw.

The total wearable energy per task is
\begin{equation}
  E_i(x_i) =
  \begin{cases}
    E^{\mathrm{local}}_i   & x_i = 0, \\[3pt]
    E^{\mathrm{offload}}_i & x_i \geq 1.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:energy_total}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Privacy Risk via Information Entropy}
\label{subsec:privacy}

Repeated offloading to a fixed node creates a predictable routing
pattern that a passive traffic-analysis attacker can exploit without
decrypting any payload. We quantify this threat
information-theoretically.

Let $p_{i,j}$ be the empirical probability that device $u_i$ routes to
node $j$, estimated over a sliding window of the most recent $W$
decisions, with $\sum_{j} p_{i,j} = 1$. The Shannon routing entropy is
\begin{equation}
  H(u_i) = -\!\sum_{\substack{j=0 \\ p_{i,j}>0}}^{M+2}
              p_{i,j} \log_2 p_{i,j},
  \label{eq:shannon_entropy}
\end{equation}
with maximum $H_{\max} = \log_2(|\set{E}|+1)$ under perfectly uniform
distribution~\cite{shannon1948}. The per-task privacy risk cost is
\begin{equation}
  R_P(u_i, x_i) = \rho_i \cdot \left(1 - \frac{H(u_i)}{H_{\max}}\right),
  \label{eq:privacy_risk}
\end{equation}
so $R_P = 0$ when routing is maximally unpredictable and $R_P = \rho_i$
when all tasks go to the same node.

\subsubsection{Privacy Guard detection rule}
\label{subsec:pg_rule}
The entropy $H(u_i)$ also feeds an explicit traffic-analysis detector
that runs in parallel with the scheduler. For each device-window, we
compute the entropy ratio
\begin{equation}
  \eta_i = \frac{H(u_i)}{H_{\max}} \in [0, 1],
  \label{eq:eta_ratio}
\end{equation}
and flag the window as a candidate attack when $\eta_i < \tau$ for a
fixed threshold $\tau$. The Guard then re-injects randomness into the
next $K$ scheduling decisions for $u_i$ to drive $\eta_i$ back up. The
threshold $\tau = 0.55$ is calibrated on a 20\% MedSec-25 hold-out
split; Section~\ref{subsec:pg} reports the resulting TPR, FPR,
precision, F1, and AUC at that operating point on the remaining 80\%.

\subsection{Patient Criticality Index and Dynamic Weight Functions}
\label{subsec:ci}

The CI module processes raw physiological streams (ECG, SpO$_2$,
blood pressure, temperature) and emits a normalized scalar
$\Phi_i \in [0,1]$, where $\Phi_i = 0$ is healthy baseline and
$\Phi_i = 1$ is life-threatening emergency. Section~\ref{sec:eval}
describes the ML model and SHAP analysis used to derive $\Phi_i$.

As criticality rises, latency must dominate over energy conservation
and privacy. We encode this shift using three continuous weight
functions:
\begin{align}
  w_E(\Phi_i) &= e^{-\alpha_E \Phi_i},
  \label{eq:weight_energy}\\[4pt]
  w_L(\Phi_i) &= \frac{e^{\beta_L \Phi_i} - 1}{e^{\beta_L} - 1},
  \label{eq:weight_latency}\\[4pt]
  w_P(\Phi_i) &= (1 - \Phi_i)^{\gamma_P},
  \label{eq:weight_privacy}
\end{align}
with shape parameters $\alpha_E = 3$, $\beta_L = 4$, $\gamma_P = 2$
(tuned via grid search; $<3\%$ cost variation across $\pm 30\%$
perturbations). The normalized weights are
\begin{equation}
  \hat{w}_k(\Phi_i) = \frac{w_k(\Phi_i)}{w_E(\Phi_i)+w_L(\Phi_i)+w_P(\Phi_i)},
  \quad k \in \{E,L,P\},
  \label{eq:weight_normalised}
\end{equation}
satisfying $\hat{w}_E + \hat{w}_L + \hat{w}_P = 1$ by construction.

\subsubsection{Cost function and constraints}

Each raw metric is min-max normalized over the candidate set
$\set{X}=\{0,1,\ldots,M+2\}$ to yield $\hat{E}_i(x)$, $\hat{T}_i(x)$,
and $\hat{R}_P(x) \in [0,1]$. The aggregate cost is
\begin{equation}
  F(x_i) =
    \hat{w}_E \cdot \hat{E}_i(x_i)
    + \hat{w}_L \cdot \hat{T}_i(x_i)
    + \hat{w}_P \cdot \hat{R}_P(x_i),
  \label{eq:cost_function}
\end{equation}
and the per-task scheduling problem is
\begin{align}
  &\minimize_{x_i}\; F(x_i) \label{eq:opt_obj}\\
  &\text{s.t.}\;\;T_i(x_i) \leq T^{\max}_i, \label{eq:sla_c}\\
  &\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;E_i(x_i) \leq E^{\mathrm{bud}}_i, \label{eq:energy_c}\\
  &\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;\;x_i \in \{0,1,\ldots,M+2\}. \label{eq:domain_c}
\end{align}

\section{The BBO-DRL Scheduler}
\label{sec:bbodrl}
%% =======================================================================

\subsection{Overview}

The constrained problem in \eqref{eq:opt_obj}--\eqref{eq:domain_c} is
NP-hard (reducible from the multi-dimensional knapsack
problem~\cite{kellerer2004knapsack}). We solve it with a two-phase
hybrid. In phase one, a DQN reads a 6D environment state and outputs
Q-values for every candidate execution node, then passes the top-$K$
nodes to phase two. In phase two, the BBO searches within that
$K$-node subspace to return the near-optimal decision $x_i^*$. This
separation exploits the DQN's temporal learning ability (it remembers
that the cloud cluster was congested for the past 200 tasks) while
using BBO's combinatorial search to find the best node within the
pre-filtered set — avoiding both the statelessness of pure BBO and the
dimensionality explosion of pure DQN.

\subsection{Bombardier Beetle Optimizer}
\label{subsec:bbo}

BBO maintains $N_{\mathrm{pop}}$ candidate solutions
$\{\vect{X}_k\}_{k=1}^{N_{\mathrm{pop}}}$, each encoding a
continuous relaxation of the offloading decision; integer $x_i$ is
recovered by projection after optimization.

\subsubsection{Chemical spray (exploration)}
\begin{equation}
  \vect{X}^{\mathrm{spray}}_k =
    \vect{X}_k
    + \vect{r}_1 \odot (\vect{X}_{\mathrm{best}} - \vect{X}_k)
    + r_2 \cdot \delta(t) \cdot \mathrm{sign}(\vect{u} - 0.5),
  \label{eq:bbo_spray}
\end{equation}
where $\vect{r}_1, \vect{u} \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)^d$, $r_2 \sim
\mathcal{U}(0,1)$, and $\odot$ is element-wise multiplication. The
spray radius decays as
\begin{equation}
  \delta(t) = \delta_0 \left(1 - \frac{t}{T_{\mathrm{BBO}}}\right)^{\!2},
  \label{eq:delta_decay}
\end{equation}
driving global search early and fine exploitation near convergence.

\subsubsection{Predator avoidance (exploitation)}
\begin{equation}
  \vect{X}^{\mathrm{avoid}}_k =
    \vect{X}_k
    + r_3 \cdot \mathrm{step}_k
      \cdot \frac{\vect{X}_{\mathrm{best}} - \vect{X}_k}
                 {\norm{\vect{X}_{\mathrm{best}} - \vect{X}_k} + \varepsilon},
  \label{eq:bbo_avoid}
\end{equation}
where $r_3 \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)$ and
$\mathrm{step}_k =
  \norm{\vect{X}_k - \vect{X}_{\mathrm{pred}}} /
  (\norm{\vect{X}_{\mathrm{best}} - \vect{X}_{\mathrm{pred}}} + \varepsilon)$
uses a stochastically placed adversarial reference point
$\vect{X}_{\mathrm{pred}}$ to diversify local search direction.

\subsubsection{Greedy selection}
\begin{equation}
  \vect{X}_k(t+1) =
  \begin{cases}
    \vect{X}^{\mathrm{spray}}_k & \text{if } F(\vect{X}^{\mathrm{spray}}_k)
                                   < F(\vect{X}^{\mathrm{avoid}}_k), \\[3pt]
    \vect{X}^{\mathrm{avoid}}_k & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:bbo_selection}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Deep Q-Network State and Reward}
\label{subsec:dqn}

The DQN observes a 6D normalized state at each scheduling epoch:
\begin{equation}
  \vect{s}_t = \bigl[\mathrm{BW}_t,\; \mathrm{CPU}_t,\; \mathrm{RTT}_t,\;
                      \mathrm{bat}_t,\; \Phi_t,\; p^{\mathrm{atk}}_t
               \bigr]^\top \in [0,1]^6,
  \label{eq:dqn_state}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{BW}_t$ is channel utilization, $\mathrm{CPU}_t$ is mean
edge/fog CPU load, $\mathrm{RTT}_t$ is measured round-trip time to the
best node, $\mathrm{bat}_t$ is wearable battery fraction, $\Phi_t$ is
the current CI, and $p^{\mathrm{atk}}_t$ is the IDS-estimated attack
probability. The action space mirrors \eqref{eq:decision_var}:
$\set{A} = \{0,1,\ldots,M+2\}$.

The CI-modulated reward is
\begin{equation}
  r_t = -(\hat{w}_E \hat{E}_t + \hat{w}_L \hat{T}_t + \hat{w}_P \hat{R}_{P,t})
         + \Phi_t \cdot \xi_{\mathrm{SLA}}(t),
  \label{eq:dqn_reward}
\end{equation}
with SLA penalty
\begin{equation}
  \xi_{\mathrm{SLA}}(t) =
  \begin{cases} -5 & \text{if } T_i(x_i) > T^{\max}_i, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases}
  \label{eq:sla_penalty}
\end{equation}
and Q-values updated via the Bellman equation
\begin{equation}
  Q(s_t, a_t) \leftarrow Q(s_t, a_t)
    + \eta\bigl[r_t + \gamma \max_{a'} Q(s_{t+1},a') - Q(s_t,a_t)\bigr],
  \label{eq:bellman}
\end{equation}
with learning rate $\eta$ and discount $\gamma$~\cite{mnih2015}.
Mini-batch sampling from experience replay buffer $\set{D}$
decorrelates training samples.

\subsection{BBO-DRL Integration}
\label{subsec:hybrid}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:bbodrl} shows the full scheduling procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{BBO-DRL Adaptive Task Scheduler}
  \label{alg:bbodrl}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \REQUIRE Task $\tau_i$, state $\vect{s}_t$, DQN parameters $\theta$,
             BBO population $\{\vect{X}_k\}$, CI $\Phi_t$
    \ENSURE Offloading decision $x_i^*$
    \STATE Observe environment state $\vect{s}_t$
           from~\eqref{eq:dqn_state}
    \STATE Compute Q-values $Q(\vect{s}_t, a)$ for all $a \in \set{A}$
    \STATE Select top-$K$ candidates:
           $\set{A}_K \leftarrow \mathrm{topK}(Q(\vect{s}_t,\cdot), K)$
    \STATE Initialize BBO population within $\set{A}_K$
    \FOR{$t = 1$ to $T_{\mathrm{BBO}}$}
      \FORALL{beetles $k = 1$ to $N_{\mathrm{pop}}$}
        \STATE Compute spray position via~\eqref{eq:bbo_spray}
        \STATE Compute avoidance position via~\eqref{eq:bbo_avoid}
        \STATE Update $\vect{X}_k$ via greedy selection~\eqref{eq:bbo_selection}
      \ENDFOR
      \STATE Update $\vect{X}_{\mathrm{best}}$
      \STATE Decay spray radius via~\eqref{eq:delta_decay}
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE $x_i^* \leftarrow \argmin_{x \in \set{A}_K} F(x)$
    \STATE Dispatch $\tau_i$ to node $x_i^*$
    \STATE Observe $\vect{s}_{t+1}$; compute $r_t$ via~\eqref{eq:dqn_reward}
    \STATE Store $(\vect{s}_t, x_i^*, r_t, \vect{s}_{t+1})$ in $\set{D}$
    \STATE Sample mini-batch from $\set{D}$; update $\theta$
           via~\eqref{eq:bellman}
    \RETURN $x_i^*$
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Per-task complexity is $\mathcal{O}(N_{\mathrm{pop}} \cdot T_{\mathrm{BBO}}
\cdot M)$, linear in the number of compute nodes and suitable for
real-time clinical deployment with $M \leq 50$.

\subsection{Simulation Setup}
\label{subsec:setup}

The simulator is a pure-NumPy event-driven environment loosely modelled
on iFogSim2~\cite{ifog2022}. It runs 10 ESP32-S3 wearables, one
Raspberry Pi 4 edge gateway, three fog nodes, and one cloud server with
the hardware parameters listed in Table~\ref{tab:hardware}. The channel
gain follows the log-distance model in~\eqref{eq:channel_gain} with
$\alpha=3$ for indoor wearable links, and queueing follows the M/M/1
model in~\eqref{eq:queue_delay}. Compute-cycle counts for the four
task types (ECG analysis, multi-vital fusion, BP analysis, SpO$_2$)
were calibrated to Cortex-M / ESP32-class published
benchmarks~\cite{banbury2021mlperf,zhang2021ecg}. ECG inference, the
dominant workload, takes 150~M cycles at 240~MHz, which is 625~ms
locally and exceeds the 500~ms deadline; this is the cause of the
Local-Only SLA violation rate reported in Section~\ref{subsec:main}.

The Monte Carlo protocol uses 30 independent replicates per
(algorithm, scale) cell with seeds $s_r = 42 + 1000 r + N$, giving
fully reproducible runs. Five task scales are tested: $N \in \{100,
500, 1000, 2000, 5000\}$. Each task stream mixes four task types
(ecg 30\%, spo2 40\%, bp 20\%, multi-vital 10\%) and three CI tiers
(low 20\%, medium 60\%, high 20\%).

DQN hyperparameters: hidden width 64, learning rate $\eta=10^{-3}$,
discount $\gamma=0.95$, replay capacity 10{,}000, mini-batch 32,
target-network sync every 50 steps. The $\varepsilon$-greedy schedule
is geometric, $\varepsilon_{t+1} = \max(\varepsilon_{\min},
0.995\,\varepsilon_t)$, with $\varepsilon_0 = 1.0$ and
$\varepsilon_{\min} = 0.05$. Under this schedule $\varepsilon$ drops
below 0.10 after 460 scheduling decisions and below 0.05 after 598;
the measured trajectory across 30 runs at $N=1000$ matches the
analytic envelope (Fig.~\ref{fig:eps}). BBO: $N_{\mathrm{pop}} = 20$,
$T_{\mathrm{BBO}} = 30$, $\delta_0 = 1.0$, $K = 3$.

\subsection{Baselines and Ablations}
\label{subsec:baselines}

Six external baselines and two internal ablations are compared against
BBO-DRL.

\textit{Local-Only} runs every task on the originating wearable.
\textit{Cloud-Only} forwards every task to the cloud server. These two
endpoints bound the trade-off space: Local-Only minimises network
exposure at the cost of battery life and high SLA violation rates;
Cloud-Only achieves the best compute throughput but concentrates all
routing on one destination, maximising privacy risk. PSO~\cite{kennedy1995pso}
re-initialises a particle swarm per task. ACO~\cite{dorigo2004aco}
uses pheromone reinforcement. HS-HHO~\cite{liu2023hshho} is the
strongest published bio-inspired hybrid for MEC scheduling and the
closest existing comparator.

\textit{PSO+DQN} is a new hybrid designed specifically to test the
source of BBO-DRL's advantage. It uses the identical DQN architecture,
state representation, reward function, and top-$K$ pre-filter as
BBO-DRL, but replaces the BBO inner search with a PSO search over the
same $K$-node candidate subspace. The PSO uses the same swarm size,
inertia weight, and iteration count as the standalone PSO baseline.
If PSO+DQN matches BBO-DRL on all metrics, the advantage belongs to
the DRL+bio-inspired coupling structure; if BBO-DRL leads on privacy,
the BBO's exploration characteristics are the decisive factor.

The two ablations isolate each BBO-DRL component individually.
\textit{BBO-only} keeps the BBO inner loop but removes the DQN
top-$K$ pre-filter, so the BBO searches over the full candidate-node
set. \textit{DQN-only} keeps the DQN and removes the BBO refinement,
taking $\arg\max_a Q(s,a)$ directly. Both use the same hyperparameters
as the full system.

high-criticality ECG tasks the system is designed to serve. Third, the
ablations confirm where the benefit comes from. BBO-only is
statistically indistinguishable from PSO and HS-HHO on every metric,
consistent with all three methods converging to the same global minimum
of $F(x)$ when there is no temporal state to exploit. DQN-only never
recovers from cold-start at this scale: 231.9~ms average latency,
6.25\% SLA violations, and the highest energy of any adaptive
scheduler. The hybrid is the only configuration that achieves the
privacy advantage together with deadline compliance.

One result in Table~\ref{tab:results} deserves a closer look: DQN-only's
35.17~mJ mean energy is $4.8\times$ BBO-DRL's 7.36~mJ and even exceeds
Cloud-Only. This is a cold-start artifact, not a system defect. With
$\varepsilon \approx 1.0$ during the first $\approx$460 scheduling
decisions, DQN-only selects actions near-randomly across all six
candidate destinations, including the cloud server. Cloud offloading
charges the wearable for the full uplink transmission at $P^t = 178$~mW
plus quiescent power $P_{\mathrm{idle}} = 33$~mW throughout the cloud
server's compute and return-path delay---a substantially longer energy
exposure than a nearby fog hop. As $\varepsilon$ decays below 0.10,
the policy converges toward energy-efficient fog routing, but the
per-run mean is taken over all 1{,}000 tasks, so the early cloud-routed
tasks pull the average up substantially. BBO-DRL avoids this because
even under $\varepsilon = 1.0$ the DQN top-$K$ pre-filter reduces the
effective action space to $K = 3$ candidates, and the BBO selects the
cost-minimising option within that restricted set, preventing the
worst cold-start routing decisions.

\subsection{CI weight scheme ablation}
\label{subsec:weights}

Section~\ref{subsec:ci} defined three CI-to-weight functions: an
exponential energy decay, a normalised-exponential latency growth, and
a polynomial privacy relaxation. To check whether the proposed shape
is doing real work, we compared it against three alternatives on
BBO-DRL at $N = 1000$ over 30 runs each:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Flat:} $\hat w_E = \hat w_L = \hat w_P = 1/3$.
  \item \textbf{Step:} $\hat w_L = 0.8$, $\hat w_E = \hat w_P = 0.1$
        for $\Phi_i > 0.5$; $\hat w_E = 0.7$, $\hat w_L = \hat w_P = 0.15$
        otherwise.
  \item \textbf{Linear:} $w_L(\Phi)=\Phi$, $w_E(\Phi)=w_P(\Phi)=
        (1-\Phi)/2$, normalised.
  \item \textbf{Non-linear} (proposed; Section~\ref{subsec:ci}).
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:weights} and Fig.~\ref{fig:weights} show the result.
Across all four schemes the four metrics fall inside an interval of
$\pm 1$ SD; the largest mean gap is on privacy risk (0.342 step vs.
0.377 flat, a 9\% spread) and the proposed non-linear scheme sits in
the middle of that band rather than at the optimum.

\subsection{Privacy Guard on MedSec-25}
\label{subsec:pg}

The Privacy Guard applies a single decision rule: flag a flow as a
candidate traffic-analysis attack when the source device's empirical
routing-entropy ratio $H(u_i) / H_{\max}$ falls below $\tau = 0.55$.
Ground truth comes from the MedSec-25 labels~\cite{medsec25}: any
flow whose mapped severity is at least 0.55 is counted as an attack
example; the rest are benign.

After parsing all 554{,}534 flows and stratified sampling to 9{,}998
events (9{,}776 attack, 222 benign across five campaign classes), the
rule produced 9{,}457 true positives, 9 false positives, 213 true
negatives, and 319 false negatives. Precision is 0.999, recall 0.967,
F1 0.983, and AUC 0.995 on the full detection channel
(Fig.~\ref{fig:roc}). An earlier draft of this paper reported recall
as 98.1\%; the correct value is 96.7\%.

  constant across all windows), causing every stateless optimizer to route all tasks to
  the cloud server on every run. Channel re-seeding changes cloud-link latency by
  $\leq 0.1\%$, which falls below display precision. This is expected behavior under
  uniform task profiles, not a precision artifact.}
\end{table}

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig10_mitbih_trace.pdf}
  \caption{MIT-BIH real-trace results (8{,}640 windows, 30 runs).
           BBO-DRL is the only adaptive scheduler that resists
           collapsing onto the cloud server when faced with uniformly
           high-CI ECG tasks.}
  \label{fig:mitbih}
\end{figure*}

The trace is harder than the synthetic stream in one specific way:
every task is ECG-class with a uniform 150~ms deadline and $\rho = 0.9$.
Under that load Local-Only fails 100\% of deadlines (625~ms local
compute exceeds 150~ms), as expected from the calibration in
Section~\ref{subsec:setup}. All stateless baselines (PSO, ACO, HS-HHO,
Cloud-Only) converge to exactly the same routing decision on every run:
forward everything to the cloud, where compute latency is lowest.
With uniform task profiles, this is the cost-minimising choice for any
stateless optimizer, and channel re-seeding has negligible effect on
cloud-link latency. The result---zero variance across all 30 runs for
those four methods (see Table~\ref{tab:mitbih}, footnote $a$)---is
expected behavior, not a code artifact. The cost is that concentrating
all routing on one destination saturates $R_P$ near $\rho_i = 0.90$.

BBO-DRL is the only adaptive scheduler that diversifies under this
pressure. Its routing entropy is high enough to drop $R_P$ to 0.451,
roughly half the stateless cluster. BBO-only reaches 0.814, partway
between BBO-DRL and the rest; that gap reflects the DQN's
reward-shaping contribution. DQN-only drops the privacy risk further
to 0.322 but pays 25~ms of additional latency. The clinical reading
is direct: on workloads dominated by sensitive, deadline-tight tasks,
the only scheduler diversifying routing---and therefore reducing
traffic-analysis exposure---is BBO-DRL.

\subsection{Pairwise significance}
\label{subsec:stats}

We tested every (algorithm, metric) pair against BBO-DRL using the
Wilcoxon rank-sum statistic on the 30 per-run samples, Bonferroni-corrected
at $20\,p$ for a family-wise error rate of $\alpha = 0.05$. All 20
tests at $N = 1000$ rejected the null at $p_{\text{corr}} < 10^{-3}$.
This holds for the cases where BBO-DRL is \emph{worse} than the
baseline---latency and energy against PSO, HS-HHO, and BBO-only---as
well as where it is better. The latency penalty is real, not noise,
and the privacy advantage is real in exactly the same statistical
sense. Once PSO+DQN results are incorporated, the family expands to
24 comparisons ($6 \times 4$) with a correspondingly stricter
per-test threshold.

\subsection{CI module interpretability}
\label{subsec:xai}

The CI module is a 100-tree Random Forest regressor (max depth 8)
trained on the Mendeley IoMT dataset with an 80/20 split. The held-out
$R^2$ is 0.825 on 10{,}000 patients. We applied SHAP's TreeExplainer
to the test set; Fig.~\ref{fig:shap} shows the mean $|\text{SHAP}|$
per feature.

%% =======================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
%% =======================================================================

The experiments tell a consistent story. At $N = 1000$, BBO-DRL
trades $\approx$9~ms of latency for a 0.08-point reduction in privacy
risk against the strongest stateless baselines, and that trade-off
widens on workloads that concentrate all tasks at high $\rho_i$, as
the MIT-BIH trace demonstrates. The DQN top-$K$ pre-filter is what
makes the trade-off possible: remove it and BBO collapses onto the
same solution as PSO. Remove the BBO and the DQN never escapes
cold-start. The coupling is the contribution.

The weight-scheme ablation in Section~\ref{subsec:weights} did not
behave as expected. We assumed the non-linear weight functions would
separate cleanly from flat and step schemes; they do not, at least
not at the 30-replicate scale on a mixed-CI workload ($p > 0.05$ for
all four metrics after Bonferroni correction). The probable cause is
that the BBO inner search dominates the per-task cost calculation, and
small differences in the weight values wash out when averaged across a
stream of mixed-criticality tasks. We would expect the separation to
appear on a workload skewed entirely to high criticality---an ICU
stream where every task arrives with $\Phi \in [0.8, 1.0]$---but that
condition has not been run as of this writing. We report the mixed-CI
negative result directly rather than hold it pending that analysis, and
will not claim the non-linear CI weight functions as an empirical
contribution until the all-high-CI evaluation is complete.

Cold-start is the practical deployment concern. The SLA violation rate
at $N = 100$ is 1.67\%, well above zero for the stateless baselines.
The straightforward remedy is offline pre-training on synthetic
trajectories drawn from the target hospital's CI distribution before
the system goes live; the Mendeley IoMT dataset provides enough data
for this. Once the replay buffer accumulates roughly 600 transitions,
$\varepsilon$ has decayed below 0.05 and the policy behaviour matches
the converged version reported at larger scales.

The MedSec-25 Privacy Guard evaluation is honest about what the rule
does and does not cover. Precision 0.999 and recall 0.967 look strong,
but the test set is heavily imbalanced (9{,}776 attack versus 222
benign flows). AUC 0.995 is the right headline figure because it
summarises the full operating-point curve rather than performance at
one threshold. The evaluation does not address an adaptive adversary
who knows the entropy threshold and deliberately injects routing
variation to defeat it; extending the Guard to that setting is the
natural next step.

Two modelling limitations deserve explicit acknowledgement. The Poisson
arrival model is analytically convenient but understates the burstiness
of real medical device traffic; an M/G/1 or MMPP/M/1 treatment would
widen the queuing delay numbers, disproportionately affecting ACO,
which is already the most queue-sensitive baseline we tested. Every
algorithm here also runs the same idealised physical layer (Shannon
capacity, log-distance path loss, no fading or retransmissions). A
real deployment would see duty-cycled wake-ups and link failures that
this simulator does not model. We are not claiming this work replaces a
field trial. The claim is narrower: the relative ordering of these
algorithms on the four headline metrics is consistent across five task
scales and two qualitatively different workloads, and that ordering
should be robust to the simulator's idealisations.

%% =======================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% =======================================================================

Among the schedulers evaluated, BBO-DRL is the only one that
actively diversifies offloading routes under high privacy sensitivity.
At $N = 1000$ across 30 Monte Carlo runs, it recorded a privacy risk
of $0.322 \pm 0.015$, against $0.398$ for PSO and HS-HHO and $0.681$
for ACO ($p_{\text{corr}} < 10^{-3}$ for every pairwise comparison,
Bonferroni-corrected across 20 hypotheses). On the MIT-BIH real-trace
evaluation, where every task carries $\rho_i = 0.9$, all stateless
baselines collapsed onto the cloud server (privacy risk 0.896--0.900);
BBO-DRL was the only adaptive method to diversify routing enough to
reach 0.451.

It does not win on latency or energy. PSO and HS-HHO are 9~ms faster
and 1.7~mJ more efficient at $N = 1000$, and the gap narrows as the
DQN converges but does not close. The BBO-only and DQN-only ablations
explain why: either component alone is insufficient, and the coupling
is what enables the privacy advantage. The proposed non-linear
CI-to-weight functions are not distinguishable from simpler schemes on
a mixed-CI workload at the 30-replicate scale; that negative result is
reported directly, and the non-linear CI weighting is not claimed as
an empirical contribution pending the all-high-CI evaluation.

The MedSec-25 Privacy Guard reached F1 = 0.983 and AUC = 0.995 at
$\tau = 0.55$. The SHAP-explained CI module achieves $R^2 = 0.825$ on
the Mendeley dataset, with a feature importance ordering (SpO$_2$,
Heart Rate, Systolic BP, Body Temperature, Diastolic BP) consistent
with sepsis-screening protocols. All code, configurations, and per-run
JSON outputs are released to make the comparison reproducible.
