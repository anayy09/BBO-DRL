# Cover Letter — IEEE Journal of Biomedical and Health Informatics

**To:** The Editor-in-Chief, IEEE Journal of Biomedical and Health Informatics
**Re:** Submission of Full-Length Original Research Article

---

We submit for your consideration the full-length original research article titled "BBO-DRL: A Hybrid Bombardier Beetle Optimizer with Deep Reinforcement Learning for Adaptive Task Offloading in IoT-Edge-Cloud Healthcare Networks" for publication in the *IEEE Journal of Biomedical and Health Informatics*, section on Computational Methods and Algorithms / Mobile and Wireless Health. This manuscript introduces a novel hybrid algorithmic framework for real-time task scheduling in Internet of Medical Things (IoMT) deployments and has not been submitted elsewhere, nor is it under concurrent review at any other venue.

The clinical imperative driving this work is the latency-criticality gap that persists in deployed IoMT infrastructures. When a wearable ECG sensor detects ventricular fibrillation or an accelerating arrhythmia episode, the window for a clinically actionable alert — one that can still influence defibrillation or pacing intervention — is measured in seconds, not minutes. At the network layer, every millisecond consumed by a suboptimal offloading decision (e.g., routing a 50 KB ECG payload to a congested cloud server when an edge gateway could classify the rhythm locally in under 20 ms) represents a direct clinical risk. Existing offloading frameworks for healthcare edge computing treat latency as one of several performance metrics rather than as a hard constraint that should dynamically govern the entire scheduling policy. Our system addresses this gap through a Criticality Index (CI) that continuously re-weights the cost function to prioritize latency above all other objectives the moment physiological telemetry indicates an acute event.

The algorithmic contribution at the core of this paper is BBO-DRL, which is, to our knowledge, the first application of the Bombardier Beetle Optimizer (BBO; arXiv:2510.17005, October 2025) to the healthcare task-offloading problem. BBO is a bio-inspired metaheuristic whose biological analogue — the bombardier beetle's dual-mode chemical defense mechanism — translates into an adaptive balance between large-radius exploration and fine-grained exploitation via antenna-length decay. While prior J-BHI publications have applied Ant Colony Optimization, Particle Swarm Optimization, and the Whale Optimization Algorithm to related scheduling problems, none have hybridized a bio-inspired explorer with a learning-based policy optimizer. The fundamental limitation BBO alone cannot overcome is statefulness: a pure metaheuristic re-optimizes from scratch at every decision epoch and cannot accumulate knowledge about channel dynamics, node load trends, or device battery trajectories over time. BBO-DRL resolves this by coupling BBO with a Deep Q-Network (DQN) whose 6-dimensional state vector encodes bandwidth utilization, CPU load, round-trip time, battery proxy, Criticality Index, and adversarial threat probability. The DQN narrows the search to a top-K candidate subspace, and BBO performs fine-grained continuous refinement within that reduced space, eliminating the DQN's curse-of-dimensionality exposure while retaining long-horizon policy learning through experience replay.

The empirical validation draws entirely on openly accessible, institutionally curated datasets, which allows complete external reproduction without any special data access agreements. The four datasets used are: the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database (PhysioNet, record set 1.0.0), which provides ground-truth arrhythmia annotations for CI calibration; the Mendeley Heterogeneous IoMT Dataset, which supplies multi-vital physiological readings across diverse patient profiles; CICIoMT2024 from the Canadian Institute for Cybersecurity, which introduces network-layer adversarial traffic for evaluating security-aware scheduling; and MedSec-25 (Kaggle: abdullah001234/medsec-25-iomt-cybersecurity-dataset), which extends adversarial coverage to emerging IoMT attack signatures. Across Monte Carlo experiments at five task-scale configurations (100 to 2,000 tasks), BBO-DRL achieves the lowest privacy risk among all adaptive algorithms — 14% below PSO and 57% below ACO at the 1,000-task reference scale. BBO-DRL also exhibits a DRL-characteristic convergence property that distinguishes it from static metaheuristics: latency decreases monotonically from 48.8 ms at N=100 to 37.9 ms at N=1,000 as the DQN policy matures, while PSO and ACO are scale-invariant. SLA violations fall from 1.40% at cold start to 0.44% at N=1,000. The per-decision computational cost is approximately 5,000 floating-point operations — well within the 1.5 GHz processing envelope of a Raspberry Pi 4 class edge gateway.

A dimension of this work that aligns directly with J-BHI's health informatics mission is the integration of Explainable AI for clinical scheduling transparency. A Random Forest regressor trained on Mendeley vital-sign features predicts the CI score, and SHAP TreeExplainer analysis reveals that SpO2 Level and Heart Rate are the two dominant drivers, accounting for the majority of predictive variance. This creates a human-interpretable audit trail: a clinician or regulator can inspect precisely which physiological signal pushed a specific task into high-priority edge execution rather than accepting a black-box scheduling decision. This property is not merely academic; forthcoming FDA guidance on Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) increasingly treats explainability as a prerequisite for clinical deployment.

We note that the three-tier architectural concept underlying this system was previously disclosed in a provisional patent application (Bio-Inspired Adaptive Task Offloading System for Energy-Efficient IoT-Edge-Cloud Healthcare Continuum), which is cited within the manuscript. The provisional patent establishes structural and functional claims at a conceptual level consistent with standard IP practice. The present manuscript represents a categorically distinct contribution: it introduces the BBO-DRL algorithm, the rigorous multi-objective mathematical model (including the M/M/1 queuing formulation, the Shannon-capacity-based transmission model, and the entropy-based privacy risk quantification), and the full empirical validation against four real-world datasets. These algorithmic and analytical contributions go substantially beyond the architectural claims of the patent filing.

All four datasets are publicly accessible at the repositories identified above; no proprietary or restricted clinical data were used. The simulation code, including all Python source files, algorithm implementations, configuration files, and figure-generation scripts, will be deposited on GitHub upon acceptance. No conflicts of interest exist among the authors. This manuscript is not under review at any other publication.
